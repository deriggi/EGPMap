Jordan Fiscal Reform II Final Report

Given its geostrategic position, resource-scarce Jordan was significantly impacted economically by the wave of the so called Arab spring revolutions and protests in 2010 and the ensuing cycle of violence that engulfed the region. Exogenous shocks to Jordan’s economy including the escalating Syrian refugee crisis as well as the disruption of Egyptian natural gas, Jordan’s most reliable source for electric power generation, have negatively impacted the energy sector especially Jordan’s National Electric Power Company (NEPCO) which, in turn, has suffered mounting operational losses. These shocks have also adversely affected the tourism sector, worker remittances and Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), which collectively resulted in an economic slowdown, a sharp increase in the Government of Jordan’s (GoJ) deficit and a significant downturn in the country’s foreign currency reserves. The difficult economic climate was further compounded by a rise in Jordanian public expectations and calls for improved government services and for more transparency and accountability in government.

In order to address the difficult socio-economic downturn, the Government of Jordan embarked on an economic and fiscal reform journey aimed at fiscal consolidation in order to decrease the government deficit and prevent debt accumulation. Accordingly, USAID-funded Fiscal Reform II Project (FRP II) kicked off in November 2009 to support the GoJ’s economic reform efforts and help build a strong fiscal foundation of Jordan’s economic growth by strengthening fiscal policy formulation, implementation and public financial management (PFM). FRP II’s technical support and assistance focused on helping the GoJ improve performance in public spending and administration of public resources, enhance accountability and transparency and increase revenue generation through sound tax policies and administration and facilitating trading across borders.
